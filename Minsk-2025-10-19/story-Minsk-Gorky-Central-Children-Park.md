### 3. The Unraveling (1:00-2:00)

Turns out? This place was founded in 1805. 1805! By Minsk's first governor, some guy named Zakhar Korneev. It was Eastern Europe's first public garden - like, the first time regular people could just walk into a designed green space. English-style landscaping, super fancy for its time.

So I'm standing here, and I'm realizing I'm not in a kids' park. I'm in a 220-year-old witness to everything that's happened in Belarus.

During Soviet times? This wasn't about ponies and paddleboats. They held poetry evenings here. Amateur theater performances. Artists setting up easels for plein air lessons. This was like... a cultural hub, you know? Where people came to think, to create, to breathe.

And then World War Two destroyed the neighborhoods around it. Just flattened them. The park expanded from 18 hectares to 28 because there was literally nothing left around it but rubble. One of those lost buildings? The house where Belarusian writer Yakub Kolas lived. Gone.

The trees I'm looking at? They watched all of that.
